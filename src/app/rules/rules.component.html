BASICS

TRANSPARENCY

In order to emphasise a consistent set of rules the GM will make an effort to be as transparent as possible when it comes to mechanics.

An example of this is checks; you will always know the general difficulty of a check before rolling, so you can be confident the GM isn’t just fiddling the number to get the result they want.

DICE

Dice are a huge part of roleplaying, introducing an element of randomness and excitement. The dice used will be standard DnD dice: d4, d6, d8, d10, d12, d20, d100. The most common usages of which are:

  • d4 - timers - sometimes the DM will roll a d4 to decide how long the players have before something happens, e.g. how many combat rounds before another horde of zombies breaks through a barricade.
  • d4-d12 - damage - these dice will be used for most damage rolls to add a level of variance to the damage being done by characters and is dependant on the weapon/ability being used.
  • d20 - Checks/saving throws
  • D100 - percentile checks for certain conditions (such as madness)

ROUNDING

When calculating a result that ends up not being a whole number, you should always round down. For example if you’re doing 11 fire damage to something with 50% fire reduction, the result would be 5 damage.

CHECKS

When performing an arbitrary action, the character may be asked to perform a check.

A check tests a character’s or monster’s innate talent and training in an effort to overcome a challenge. The GM calls for an ability check when a character or monster attempts an action (other than an attack) that has a chance of failure. When the outcome is uncertain, the dice determine the results.

For every ability check, the GM decides which of the six stats is relevant to the task at hand and the difficulty of the task, represented by a Difficulty Class. The more difficult a task, the higher its DC:

Difficulty DC
Trivial 5
Easy 10
Medium 20
Hard 30
Very hard 40
Nearly impossible 50+

To make an ability check, roll a d20 and add the relevant ability modifier. As with other d20 rolls, apply bonuses and penalties, and compare the total to the DC. If the total equals or exceeds the DC, the ability check is a success—the creature overcomes the challenge at hand. Otherwise, it’s a failure, which means the character or monster makes no progress toward the objective or makes progress combined with a setback determined by the GM. Some checks may require teamwork (for example pushing a very large object), in this case, players can roll together to combine into a stronger check.

DEATH

It will be possible to die. It is your responsibility as players (as you do in real life) to try and avoid getting into situations where death is likely.

There are ways to recover from death however these will only be available to a limited degree at higher levels (such as rare magical artifacts or high level cleric spells). If a character dies without a way to recover, they are gone for good and the player will need to start a fresh character.

Whenever you start your turn with 0 health, you must make a special check, called a death saving throw, to determine whether you creep closer to death or hang onto life. Unlike other saving throws, this one isn’t tied to any ability score. You are in the hands of fate now, aided only by spells and features that improve your chances of succeeding on a saving throw.

Roll a d20: If the roll is 10 or higher, you succeed. Otherwise, you fail. A success or failure has no effect by itself. On your third success, you become stable (see below). On your third failure, you die. The successes and failures don’t need to be consecutive; keep track of both until you collect three of a kind. The number of both is reset to zero when you regain any hit points or become stable.

Rolling 1 or 20: When you make a death saving throw and roll a 1 on the d20, it counts as two failures. If you roll a 20 on the d20, you regain 1 hit point.

Damage at 0 Hit Points: If you take any damage while you have 0 hit points, you suffer a death saving throw failure. If the damage is from a critical hit, you suffer two failures instead. If the damage equals or exceeds your hit point maximum, you suffer instant death.

A character who is healed above 0 health is considered immediately conscious and no longer dying.

Instant Death

Instant death can occur when a player takes damage equal to 100% of their health below 0. For example if a character with 20 max health gets hit for 40 damage, they would instantly be dead.

Stabilizing

When a character is stable, they are still considered unconscious and at 0 health, however they are no longer dying. Characters can be stabilised by other players through the use of bandages, and in some cases resourceful role play actions such as putting pressure on blood flow etc.

CHARACTER CREATION

Creating a character consists of the following steps:

  • Select a Race
  • Select a Class
  • Allocate your Primary Stats
  • Enter additional detail such as: name, age, appearance, personality, background, aims

RACES

Your race affects your appearance, how some characters judge you, and has an effect on your starting stats. Each race also has an additional unique passive ability.

Human

The most abundant race in the world of Solusek, humans are incredibly varied and flexible. They stand on average between 5' and 6'6" tall.

Stat Bonuses

None

Flexible

Humans start with an additional 5 stat points to spend at creation

High Elf

The proudest and most beautiful of all the elven races, high elves are a usually good-natured and caring, though do quite often see themselves as a superior race. They are generally pale-skinned and stand on average between 5'5" and 6'6" tall.

Stat Bonuses

+2 to Intelligence, +2 to Spirit, +1 to Charisma, -1 to Strength

Meditation

When taking a short rest, the high elf may refresh one 'once per day' ability.

Wood Elf

The most neutral of the three elven races, wood elves like to keep to themselves and are most happy when at one with nature. They tend to have tanned skin and stand on average between 4'5" and 5'9" tall.

Stat Bonuses

+2 to Agility, +1 to Constitution, +1 to Spirit

Quickstep

+2 to movement

Dark Elf

Often considered to be selfish and malicious, Dark elves are not well liked by the other elves. They have dark blue/black skin and stand on average between 4'5" and 5'9" tall.

Stat Bonuses

+2 to Agility, +2 to Intelligence

Merciless

+1 on all attack rolls against wounded targets (increases to +2 at level 5, +3 at level 10, +4 at level 11 and +5 at level 20)

Halfling

The smallest people with the biggest personalities, halflings are generally friendly and optimistic. They stand on average between 3' and 4'5" tall.

Stat Bonuses

+2 to Agility, +1 to Constitution, +1 to Charisma

Tiny

+1 to all dodge rolls (increases to +2 at level 5, +3 at level 10, +4 at level 15, +5 at level 20)

Dwarf

Dwarves are known for their love of treasure, beer and facial hair. The are only slightly taller than halflings on average but are generally much stockier.

Stat Bonuses

+2 to Constitution, +1 to Strength, +1 to Spirit

Limitless

+5 to health per level

Ogre

The largest (and heaviest) of the races, ogres typically posses great strength and not-so-great intellect. They are generally hairless and stand between 7'5 and 9' tall.

Stat Bonuses

+3 to Strength, +2 to Constitution, -1 to Intelligence

Big Boned

Immune to physical stun and knockdown

Troll

The green and slender cousin to the ogres, trolls are feared for their ability to regenerate quickly in combat. They stand between 7'5 and 9' tall.

Stat Bonuses

+2 to Strength, +2 to Constitution, -1 to Intelligence, -1 to Charisma

Regenerate

Regenerate 1 health at the end of each combat round (increases to 2 at level 3, 3 at level 6, 4 at level 9, 5 at level 12, 6 at level 15, and 7 at level 18)

Iksar

The most hated race in Solusek for their part in starting The Great War, Iksar are easily recognisable by their scaled, lizard-like skin. Said to be descended from the mating between ancient dragons and humans.

Stat Bonuses

+2 to Strength, +2 to Constitution, -1 to Intelligence, -1 to Charisma

Scaled

+1 to armour (increases to +2 at level 5, +3 at level 10, +4 at level 15 and +5 at level 20)

CLASSES

Your class defines how you operate in combat, giving you access to a specific set of equipment and abilities. Each class also has a Hit Die, which controls how much health they have per level.

The class abilities are still under construction, however what is displayed here should give you a feel for how the class is played.
