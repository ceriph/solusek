Basics

This custom rule set is based on DnD 5th Edition with some alterations. The alterations are based around theming, simplicity, and slightly less randomness.

Dice

Dice are a large part of DnD, introducing an element of randomness and excitement. The dice used will be standard DnD dice: d4, d6, d8, d10, d12, d20, d100. The most common usages of which are:

  • d4 - timers - sometimes the DM will roll a d4 to decide how long before something happens
  • d4-d12 - damage - used for damage rolls
  • d20 - checks/saving throws
  • d100 - percentile checks for certain conditions such as madness

ROUNDING

When calculating a result that ends up not being a whole number, you should always round down.

CHECKS

When performing an arbitrary action, the character may be asked to perform a check.

A check tests a character’s innate talent and training in an effort to overcome a challenge. The GM calls for an ability check when a character attempts an action (other than an attack) that has a chance of failure. When the outcome is uncertain, the dice determine the results.

For every ability check, the GM decides which of the six stats is relevant to the task at hand and the difficulty of the task, represented by a Difficulty Class. You will be informed of the general difficulty of the task before attempting to do it. The more difficult a task, the higher its DC:

Difficulty DC
Trivial 5
Easy 10
Medium 20
Hard 30
Very hard 40
Nearly impossible 50+

To make an ability check, roll a d20 and add the relevant ability modifier. As with other d20 rolls, apply bonuses and penalties, and compare the total to the DC. If the total equals or exceeds the DC, the ability check is a success—the creature overcomes the challenge at hand. Otherwise, it’s a failure, which means the character or monster makes no progress toward the objective or makes progress combined with a setback determined by the GM. Some checks may require teamwork (for example pushing a very large object), in this case, players can roll together to combine into a stronger check.

Resting

Characters can rest out of combat to gain health and refresh certain abilities. There are two types of rests:

Short Rest

A short rest is considered a 1 hour break that the characters can use after combat to recover health. Shorts rests should only be taken when the characters are not currently in any immediate danger.

If the characters are not exhausted and have food and water, they roll their class hit die and gain health equal to the result + their constitution + their level.

Characters can take 3 short rests per day before becoming exhausted.

Long Rest

A long rest is considered a full nights sleep taking place in a prepared camp or lodgings. Provided the characters have access to food and water, they regain all hit points, refresh all abilities and remove any existing exhausted conditions.

Combat

Combat consists of the Setup phase, and the Turns phase.

Setup

During setup, the players and DM determine surprise, positions and initiative.

Surprise

The GM will decide if either side is surprised, in which case the other side gets a free round of turns before the next phase begins. An example would be sneaking up on a camp of sleeping goblins.

Initiative

All characters must roll initiative. To do this they roll 1d20 and add their movement stat. The turn order is then determined starting with highest initiative down to lowest.

Position

Position on the battlefield is then determined if necessary, dependant on situation. In some cases position may already be evident.

Turns

Once setup is completes the turns phase begins. Each character, in order of initiative, takes their turn. When the last character has taken their turn the round is over and a new one begins back with highest initiative character. This process repeats until the battle is over.

On a characters turn, they may perform one movement, and one action. These can be in any order, so the character may make their attack then retreat if they wish.

Movement

When performing their movement, they may move the number of squares (meters) equal to their movement stat. Diagonal movement is allowed. Friendly figures do not block movement, however enemy figures do block movement.

Action

For their action, characters have the choice of attack, skill, stabilize and equip.

Attack

The character can make a basic attack with their main weapon. This consists of the steps roll attack, compare to avoidance rating, if the attack is equal to or higher than avoidance then the character also rolls for damage, subtracts the targets mitigation, then if any damage is left over health is removed from the target.

Take for example Dave the quiet warrior, he is level 1, has 3 agility and 2 strength wielding a damaged short sword, and is attacking a level 1 goblin with 12 dodge, 1 armour and and 8 health:

Attack Roll .. 1d20 + AGI .. 1d20 + 3 .. 11 + 3 .. 14
Compare to dodge rating .. 14 vs 12 .. hit!
Damage Roll .. 1d6 - 2 + 2 .. 3 - 2 + 2 .. 3
Subtract Armour .. 3 - 1 .. 2
Remove health .. 8 - 2 .. 6

Remember to use the correct types of avoidance and mitigation for the attack type!

Skill

The character can choose to use a skill of type 'action'. These will have varying effects which will be described within the skill text. Many skills are also attacks and will follow the same process above.

Equip

The character can unequip or equip something from their inventory, such as swapping out a shield for another weapon.

Stabilize

The character can use bandages to attempt to stabilize a dying character. They must roll a 1d20 and on 11 or above, the target is considered stabilized.

Instant

At any time during the Turns phase (not only on their own turn) characters may interrupt to perform any skills they have with the type 'instant'.

Critical Hits

When a character rolls a natural 20, as in their d20 roll without modifiers is 20, they score a hit regardless of the targets avoidance rating. They also do not need to roll any damage dice, assuming full damage.

DEATH

It will be possible to die, as a player it is your responsibility as players to avoid putting your characters in situations where death is likely. It is possible to recover from death through rare magical equipment or high level Cleric spells, but these will be unavailable for some time.

Dying

Whenever you start your turn with 0 health, you must make a special check, called a death saving throw, to determine whether you creep closer to death or hang onto life. Unlike other saving throws, this one isn’t tied to any ability score. You are in the hands of fate now, aided only by spells and features that improve your chances of succeeding on a saving throw.

Roll a d20: If the roll is 10 or higher, you succeed. Otherwise, you fail. A success or failure has no effect by itself. On your third success, you become stable (see below). On your third failure, you die. The successes and failures don’t need to be consecutive; keep track of both until you collect three of a kind. The number of both is reset to zero when you regain any hit points or become stable.

Rolling 1 or 20: When you make a death saving throw and roll a 1 on the d20, it counts as two failures. If you roll a 20 on the d20, you regain 1 hit point.

Damage at 0 Hit Points: If you take any damage while you have 0 hit points, you suffer a death saving throw failure. If the damage is from a critical hit, you suffer two failures instead. If the damage equals or exceeds your hit point maximum, you suffer instant death.

A character who is healed above 0 health is considered immediately conscious and no longer dying.

Instant Death

Instant death can occur when a player takes damage which would reduce them to their maximum health below 0. This can occur on any player regardless of whether they are already dying, for example if a character with 20/20 max health gets hit for 40 damage, they would instantly be dead.

For this reason extra care should be taken with characters or creatures that are far more powerful than you. Provoking an attack from them would not be wise!

Stabilizing

When a character is stable, they are still considered unconscious and at 0 health, however they are no longer dying. Characters can be stabilised by other players through the use of bandages, and in some cases resourceful role play actions such as putting pressure on blood flow etc.

Character Advancement

Characters advance in level by earning experience points. These are awarded for various actions such as defeating enemies in combat, completing a quest, or exceptional feats of roleplaying.

When advancing in level, characters will improve their stats and unlock abilities dependant on their class. The general rule is that on even levels, the character gains a bonus stat, and on odd levels they gain a new ability.

Inspiration

Inspiration is a rule the game master can use to reward you for playing your character in a way that’s true to his or her personality traits, ideal, bond, and flaw. By using inspiration, you can draw on your personality trait of compassion for the downtrodden to give you an edge in negotiating with the Beggar Prince. Or inspiration can let you call on your bond to the defense of your home village to push past the effect of a spell that has been laid on you.

Gaining Inspiration

Your GM can choose to give you inspiration for a variety of reasons. Typically, GMs award it when you play out your personality traits, give in to the drawbacks presented by a flaw or bond, and otherwise portray your character in a compelling way. Your GM will tell you how you can earn inspiration in the game.

You either have inspiration or you don’t — you can’t stockpile multiple “inspirations” for later use.

Using Inspiration

If you have inspiration, you can expend it when you make an Attack roll, saving throw, or ability check. Spending your inspiration gives you advantage on that roll.

Additionally, if you have inspiration, you can reward another player for good roleplaying, clever thinking, or simply doing something exciting in the game. When another player character does something that really contributes to the story in a fun and interesting way, you can give up your inspiration to give that character inspiration.

Advantage (and disadvantage)

Sometimes a Special ability or spell tells you that you have advantage or disadvantage on an ability check, a saving throw, or an Attack roll. When that happens, you roll a second d20 when you make the roll. Use the higher of the two rolls if you have advantage, and use the lower roll if you have disadvantage. For example, if you have disadvantage and roll a 17 and a 5, you use the 5. If you instead have advantage and roll those numbers, you use the 17.

If multiple situations affect a roll and each one grants advantage or imposes disadvantage on it, you don’t roll more than one additional d20. If two favorable situations grant advantage, for example, you still roll only one additional d20.

If circumstances cause a roll to have both advantage and disadvantage, you are considered to have neither of them, and you roll one d20. This is true even if multiple circumstances impose disadvantage and only one grants advantage or vice versa. In such a situation, you have neither advantage nor disadvantage.

You usually gain advantage or disadvantage through the use of skills or inspiration. The GM can also decide that circumstances influence a roll in one direction or the other and grant advantage or impose disadvantage as a result.

STATS

Stats are the numbers which make up a character's ability to excel (or not) at certain things. This is split up into primary stats and secondary stats.

PRIMARY

Primary stats are the building blocks of your character. All characters start with 1 in each stat, adjusted by your selected race, plus 5 points (10 for humans) to spend as you like.

At creation only, no individual base stat (not including racial bonuses) can be raised above 5.

The class you choose should guide you in which stats to allocate points in, however no matter what class you choose it is recommended to place a point or two in Constitution for health and Agility for movement.

Strength [STR]

How physically strong the character is. Allows the character to flex and lift heavy objects, as well as do more melee damage in combat.

Constitution [CON]

How healthy and fit the character is. Directly affects the health of a character as well as resistance to poison and disease.

Agility [AGI]

How fast and coordinated the character is. Directly affects the movement and dodge ratings of a character. It also affects damage done using ranged weapons.

Intelligence [INT]

How witty and educated the character is. Directly affects the power of most arcane, fire, ice and dark based magic, as well as resistance to those.

Spirit [SPI]

How in tune a character is with either their inner spirit or their god. Directly affects to power of most light and energy magic, as well as resistance to those.

Charisma [CHA]

How charming and persuasive a character is. Directly affects the power of most mind magic as well as resistance to it. Also useful when trying to pick up girls.

SECONDARY

Secondary stats are calculated from both the characters primary stats, level, and any item enhancements. They cannot be directly modified in the same way that the primary stats are.

Health

The general well-being of a character, health is used as an indicator of how close a character is to dieing. It is based on constitution, level, and the class. All characters start with health equal to:

Max Health = Hit Die Max + Constitution

Each level characters gain health equal to the average of their class hit die plus constitution:

Bonus Health = Hit Die Average + Constitution

Note than any increases in Constitution are applied retroactively, so the full formula to calculate how much maximum health a character currently has is:

Max Health = Hit Die Max + Constitution + ((Hit Die Average + Constitution) * (Level - 1))

For example a level 5 Warrior (class hit die d12) with 5 Constitution, and a level 5 Wizard (class hit die d6) with 3 Constitution would have:

Warrior = 12 + ((6 + 5) * 4) = 56
Wizard = 6 + ((3 + 3) * 4) = 30

When a character is below 50% of their maximum health, they are considered wounded.

When a character is at 0 health, they are considered unconscious and dying (unless stabilized). For more detail see the section on Death.

Temporary Health

Some spells and abilities grant a character temporary health, which acts as a temporary buffer against damage. Characters with full health can be given additional health via temporary health.

When a character with temporary health takes damage, the damage is first taken from temporary health and if any is leftover it is then taken on actual health.

Once temporary health is removed, it cannot be regained via healing. If a character with temporary health is granted more temporary health, they are not combined, the character must chose whether to keep their existing pool or to take the new one.

When a character takes any sort of rest, temporary hit points are removed.

Movement

This is the characters ability to move around the battlefield in combat. Units are generally considered to be in meters, so a character with 5 movement is able to move 5 meters per round.

Characters have movement equal to their Agility stat plus any modifiers. It should be noted that heavy armour and shields negatively affect a characters movement. A character will always have a minimum of 1 movement regardless of modifiers.

Movement is also used to decide combat turn order during the Initiative phase.

Avoidance

The characters ability to avoid attacks, and comes in two categories: dodge and resistance.

Dodge affects the characters ability to avoid physical attacks as well as some projectile based magic. To calculate a characters Dodge Rating, take 10 and add their Agility, then any other modifiers. Similar to movement, dodge is negatively affected by heavy armour and shields.

Resistance is a general term that affects the characters ability to avoid poison, disease and magical attacks. The specific stat used depends type of magic being employed and will be described on the ability/weapon.

Mitigation

The characters ability to reduce damage they taken from hits. Like avoidance this comes in two categories: armour and magic reduction.

Armour reduces the amount of physical damage taken and is generally improved by wearing better and heavier armour.

Magic Reduction reduces the amount of magical damage taken and can only be improved through certain abilities and magical items. Magic damage can come in many different subtypes: fire, ice, arcane, dark, light and energy. Note that if something states 'Magic Reduction', it applies to all types of magical damage, however some abilities and items may only apply to certain types of magic, in this case it will be specific, such as 'Fire Reduction'.

Damage from poison and disease cannot be mitigated.

ADDITIONAL INFO

To finalise your character, you should give them some general details such as name, age, appearance, personality, background, and life goals.

For the background and life goals you can be an in depth of as mysteriously vague as you wish. You do not need to disclose all these details with the other players, however being open with the DM allows them to mould the story to the characters.

Conditions

There are many conditions throughout the world which can have both positive and negative effects for a given duration.

Charmed

Charmed characters have had their mind enslaved and must obey the wishes of their master for the duration.

Dead

Characters are dead when they either fail 3 death saving throws or suffer instant death. Dead characters are gone, however there are some magical artifacts and spells which can bring dead characters back to life within a short duration of their death.

Dying

Characters at 0 health which have not yet been stabilized are considered to be dying, and must make a death saving throw each turn. Dying characters are always considered unconscious.

Diseased

Diseases come in many forms, all of which have negative effects, weakening the character in some way. While diseased, the character must make a CON saving throw at the start of each turn against the DC described, if they succeed they are no longer diseased, if they fail the disease continues and they must take the effects described.

Exhausted

A character who is exhausted can no longer gain the benefits of a short rest and must take a long rest in order to recover from the condition.

Invisible

Invisible characters cannot be seen or attacked, except by characters with the See Invisible trait.

Knocked down

Characters who are knocked down receive a -5 modifier to their dodge rating and their next movement must be spent getting back up, after which they can continue to perform an action as normal.

Mad

Madness can affect characters in many ways, from a minor form of OCD to mistaking teammates for enemies in battle. The type and duration of madness will be defined as and when it occurs.

Rooted

Rooted characters are held in place by some force and cannot move for the duration. They can still perform other actions as normal.

Stabilized

Characters are considered stabilized if they are at 0 health but are no longer dying. They are still considered unconscious.

Stunned

Stunned characters suffer a -5 modifier to their dodge rating and cannot move or perform any actions on their turn, however they are still aware of their surroundings.

Poisoned

Poisoned characters take damage at the end of each turn for the duration. The damage taken depends on the type of the poison and will be detailed. While poisoned, the character must make a CON saving throw at the start of each turn against the DC described, if they succeed they are no longer poisoned, if they fail the poison continues and they must take the damage described.

Wounded

Characters are considered wounded when they are at less than 50% of their maximum health. Some abilities will only be in effect when a character or target is wounded.

Unconscious

Characters are considered unconscious when their health is at 0, and they suffer a -5 to their dodge rating. Unconscious characters are unaware of their surroundings and cannot move or take any actions on their turn.

Equipment

This section contains details on all the basic equipment available to the characters. In addition to the equipment stated on their selected class, all characters start with a set of bandages each, as well as 5 units of food and water each.

Equipment can be bought, acquired via gifts for completed quests, or hunting treasure.

Currency

The unit of currency in Solusek is gold coins. For this campaign, the characters always start with no gold coins, so it is in their interest to pursue avenues of income.

Gold can be obtained by completing quests, stealing, killing, or hunting and selling treasure.

Quality

All weapons and armour come in a specific range of qualities which affect the modifier on the item, for weapons this effects the attack and damage rolls, for armour it affects the armour it provides.

The available qualities are:

  • Damaged - the item is damaged and suffers a -2 modifier
  • Weathered - the item has seen some use and suffers a -1 modifier
  • Fine - the item is of acceptable quality, and has no modifiers
  • Imbued - the item has been imbued with magic, it has a +1 modifier and another magical effect
  • Legendary - the item is a unique artifact, it has a +2 modifier and multiple magical effects

Higher quality items are harder to obtain and cost more money to buy.
